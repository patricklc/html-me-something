Introduction

In the summer of 2006, my family and I embarked on a week-long trip of Ireland, visiting many urban and rural attractions on the west side of the country. There were many things to get used to in this foreign land, like the euro (€) and driving differences. However, the most enduring memories came from the places built hundreds or thousands of years ago, transporting the viewer back in time. The following describes the most notable memories from the trip.

Two Midwests

As we stepped out of the Shannon Airport in mid-west Ireland, the foreignness of the country wasn't yet apparent. However, once we climbed into the little Peugeot and set out to our first destination, the differences became obvious. Whether it was street signs in Gaelic, rolling rocky hills on each side of the narrow road, sheep grazing on those hills, abandoned castles scattered throughout the countryside. Much of the trip I spent in the backseat of our rented charcoal Pugeot, listening to music and astonishedly staring at the endless, beautiful countryside.

Ancestors

I never knew my great-grandmother. She lived her whole life in Ireland. However, we visited the church where she was baptized and likely buried. The church was long abandoned. The only sign of life was cows grazing in a neighboring field. It was a reminder of how many people left the country for a better life.

Castles & Other Historical Sites

Everywhere we went there were castles. Along the roadway there were walls of stone. The boundaries of fields were clearly marked with stone. Protecting property and wealth has obviously been very important to the Irish. The differnce between Ireland and other places is that much of the landscape hasn't changed since medieval times. Newer buildings and infrastructure have been added but the history is alive and on display.

Glorious Garden

One of the most impressive sites was the Muckross House and Gardens, a Victorian mansion at what is now Killarney National Park. The garden was staretd in preparation for Queen Victoria's visit and was revived and expanded in the early twentieth century. Among the most stunning plants was the huge Rhodendron.

Fond Farewell

It was definitely a memorable experience, going to a country that I grew up hearing and reading about. It was an interesting glimpse of the past. Much of what I saw looked like it hadn't changed for centuries or maybe even milennia. The stone castle, churches and walls, grazing animals, tombs, cemeteries, even the Victiorian mansion. It's comforting to know that the past is still alive in the land of my ancestors.
